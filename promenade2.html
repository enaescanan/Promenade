is a game where the most willing to perform wins.

players advance on the board only after revealing tightly-held truths or performing bold dares and having their fellow players judge them. winners get crowned prom royalty!

how do i play?

print out your doll and use it as your piece on the board (tip: tape it to a toothpick then stick that toothpick onto a small paper cube to get it to stay upright).

get the cards, board, and die ready. determine the order of turns.

roll the die to start off the game.

each player gets one turn, and can move in any direction per turn.

if you land on a place without a number, you end your turn on that space.

if you land on a place with a number, pick the card that corresponds to that number. each card will have a “truth” half and a “dare” half, and you will have to accomplish one to keep moving.

doing your “truth” or “dare” right will let you move forward the amount of spaces noted on the card. doing it wrong will force you to move back the amount of spaces noted on the card. whether or not you do it right depends on your friends. your friends can also determine which direction you move in going backwards (you get to choose going forwards).

you can repeat cards. on your second round with a card, do the option that you did not previously do. if you have already done both the “truth” or the “dare,” you can do either one again.

the first to reach the center (#27) and get crowned prom royalty wins. the game ends when someone wins.

lately, more and more people have been considering their gender something they can personally discover and label as they wish. instead of understanding gender as a social category based on biological sex, many people tend to think of gender as something they experience as individuals.

these new genders are as real as they are to the person who experiences them (in other words, real enough). however, they may not have weight as social categories.

since these genders are indeed new, we don’t have any social framework for them yet, and in fact we might never have one. people’s new, non-binary genders may not yet have any bearing on what those people are expected to do or be in the social world the way the genders “man” and “woman” do.

you don’t have to label your gender, but it’s ok if you want to. you might decide to use a general label that doesn’t describe your gender in detail, or you might decide to look for a label that is more specific, including your mode of expression.

if you want to label your gender, you can read articles or watch videos by gender-variant individuals and see what terms they use, or look up resources about gender (there are some linked below).

if you don’t want to label your gender, you can just… not! you can experience and express your gender without strictly naming what it is or describing in detail what it means to you.

even if you don’t understand it or agree with it, it’s not your duty or right to tell gender-variant and transgender people they are incomprehensible to you or wrong. keep these thoughts to yourself when you are around them as basic courtesy, and listen to what they tell you about themselves.
